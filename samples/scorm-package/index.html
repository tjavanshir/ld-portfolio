Writing Effective Learning Objectives

Section 1 — Why Objectives Matter

The Purpose of Learning Objectives

A learning objective is not a course title or a topic description. It's a precise, measurable statement of what a learner will be able to do as a result of instruction.

Objectives determine content selection, sequence, instructional strategies, and assessment methods. If you can't link a piece of content back to an objective, it probably shouldn't be there.

When learners know exactly what they should be able to do by the end, they can focus their attention and self-monitor their progress. Clear objectives reduce anxiety and improve engagement.

A well-written objective already contains the assessment criteria. If the objective says "demonstrate correct emergency shutdown procedure," you know exactly what the assessment must require.

Measurable objectives allow organisations to evaluate training effectiveness against business outcomes — the basis of Kirkpatrick Level 4 and ROI evaluation.

Section 2 — Bloom's Taxonomy

Bloom's Revised Taxonomy

Bloom's Taxonomy (revised by Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) provides a hierarchy of cognitive skills to target in learning objectives — from simple recall to complex creation.

Level 1 — Remember

Recall facts and basic concepts from memory.

  • Verbs: define, list, recall, recognise, state, identify, name, repeat
  • Example objective: "List the five phases of the ADDIE model."

Level 2 — Understand

Explain ideas or concepts in own words.

  • Verbs: explain, describe, summarise, paraphrase, classify, interpret
  • Example objective: "Explain the difference between formative and summative assessment."

Level 3 — Apply

Use information in new situations.

  • Verbs: use, demonstrate, implement, execute, solve, perform
  • Example objective: "Demonstrate correct hand hygiene technique using the WHO 6-step method."

Level 4 — Analyse

Draw connections and break down information.

  • Verbs: compare, differentiate, examine, organise, distinguish, attribute
  • Example objective: "Compare SAM and ADDIE methodologies across three design criteria."

Level 5 — Evaluate

Justify decisions and make judgements.

  • Verbs: judge, critique, justify, defend, assess, prioritise, recommend
  • Example objective: "Justify the selection of synchronous vs asynchronous delivery for a given audience."

Level 6 — Create

Produce original work by combining elements.

  • Verbs: design, develop, compose, construct, produce, plan, build
  • Example objective: "Design a complete needs analysis plan for a new onboarding program."
Section 3 — The ABCD Model

Writing with the ABCD Model

The ABCD model gives every learning objective a consistent, complete structure. Each component adds clarity and makes the objective easier to assess.

A — Audience

Who is the learner? Be specific: "new employees," "team leaders," "nursing students."

B — Behaviour

What will they do? Use a measurable Bloom's Taxonomy verb. Avoid "understand" or "know."

C — Condition

Under what circumstances? "Given a case study," "using the LMS," "without reference materials."

D — Degree

How well or how many? "with 90% accuracy," "within 3 minutes," "for all four scenarios."

Before & After Examples
❌

"Learners will understand workplace safety policies."

✅

"By the end of this course, new employees (A) will be able to identify (B) the correct PPE for each of the five listed hazardous work areas, without assistance (C), with 100% accuracy (D)."
