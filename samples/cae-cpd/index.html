Lesson 1 — What Is C1 Advanced?

Cambridge English C1 Advanced (CAE) is an internationally recognised qualification certifying English at CEFR Level C1. It is accepted by thousands of universities, employers, and immigration authorities worldwide — including for student visas to the UK and Australia.

Who takes it?

  • Learners applying to study at English-medium universities
  • Professionals needing certified advanced English for employment
  • Individuals seeking immigration status requiring English evidence
  • Learners wanting formal proof of C1 proficiency for career or academic progression

C1 on the CEFR scale

A1
Beginner
A2
Elementary
B1
Intermediate
B2
Upper-Int.
C1
Advanced ⭐
C2
Mastery

At C1, learners can understand a wide range of demanding, longer texts and recognise implicit meaning. They can express themselves fluently and spontaneously without much obvious searching for expressions — the kind of language use real-world professional and academic contexts demand.

Paper-based vs Computer-based

C1 Advanced is available in both formats. Content and marking are identical; the Speaking test is always face-to-face. For the computer-based version, students benefit from practising scrolling long texts on screen and using the live word-count tool in Writing.

💡

Teacher note

The computer-based version does not change what you teach — only how students interact with the interface. Module 4 covers computer-based preparation in detail.

Lesson 2 — Exam Structure: All Four Papers

The C1 Advanced exam consists of four papers. The Reading & Use of English paper carries double the weight of each of the others.

✍️ Writing
🎧 Listening

The Reading & Use of English paper has 8 parts. Parts 1–4 focus on grammar and vocabulary; Parts 5–8 focus on reading comprehension. Click each part below to learn what it tests:

Part 1 — Multiple Choice Cloze (8 marks)▼

A text with 8 gaps, each followed by four options (A–D). Tests vocabulary — idioms, fixed phrases, collocations, and phrasal verbs.

Teaching focus: Build collocation awareness. Students must read the full sentence before choosing. Distractors often share similar meanings but wrong collocations (e.g. do vs make + noun).

Part 2 — Open Cloze (8 marks)▼

A text with 8 gaps where candidates write one word. Tests grammar and lexico-grammar — prepositions, articles, pronouns, conjunctions, auxiliaries.

Teaching focus: Dependent prepositions, fixed expressions, discourse markers. Regular gap-fill with authentic texts is most effective. Spelling must be correct.

Part 3 — Word Formation (8 marks)▼

A text with 8 gaps. A root word in capitals is provided; candidates form the correct word (using prefixes, suffixes, negation, compounding).

Teaching focus: Systematic study of affixes; word family grids. Students must identify the grammatical category needed (noun/verb/adjective/adverb) before forming the word. Negative prefixes are frequently required.

Part 4 — Key Word Transformations (12 marks)▼

6 items. A sentence plus key word, then candidates rewrite using 3–6 words including the key word so it means the same thing.

Marks: 2 per item (0, 1, or 2). Examiners assess two halves of each rewritten sentence independently — partial credit is possible.

Teaching focus: Common structures: passives, reported speech, conditionals, comparatives, modals, phrasal verbs. Transformation drills are essential practice.

Part 5 — Multiple Choice Reading (12 marks)▼

A long text of around 750 words with 6 questions (A–D). Tests detailed comprehension, opinion, attitude, purpose, and implicit meaning.

Teaching focus: The correct answer is usually a paraphrase of the text — train students not to choose answers based on matching vocabulary alone. Distractors often contain words from the text that misrepresent the meaning.

Part 6 — Cross-Text Multiple Matching (8 marks)▼

Four short texts (different writers on the same theme) plus 4 questions identifying which writer expresses a given view, or where writers agree or disagree.

Teaching focus: Often the hardest part for students. Teach text-coding: mark each writer's stance on each sub-topic. The skill is cross-referencing between texts, not finding information within one text.

Part 7 — Gapped Text (12 marks)▼

A long text with 6 paragraphs removed and placed below in jumbled order. Candidates match removed paragraphs back to gaps. One paragraph is a distractor.

Teaching focus: Lexical chains, reference words (pronouns, demonstratives), and discourse connectors are the key signals. Teach students to read what comes before AND after each gap and study the internal logic of each removed paragraph.

Part 8 — Multiple Matching (10 marks)▼

One or more texts with 10 items. Candidates identify which section contains the information described. Sections may be used more than once.

Teaching focus: A scanning task. Students should read all 10 items first, identify keywords, then scan efficiently. Answers are always paraphrases — identical wording is never reproduced.

Lesson 3 — Cambridge Scale & Grading

Cambridge English uses the Cambridge Scale (160–210). Scores from all papers are combined and converted using statistical equating, ensuring comparability across exam sessions.

GradeScale ScoreCEFROutcome
Grade A200–210C2Certificate awarded — performance at C2 noted
Grade B193–199C1C1 Advanced certificate awarded
Grade C180–192C1C1 Advanced certificate awarded (minimum pass)
Grade D173–179B2Cambridge B2 certificate — C1 not achieved
Grade EBelow 173Below B2No certificate awarded
💡

Grade A explained

A student scoring Grade A does NOT receive a separate C2 Proficiency certificate. They receive a C1 Advanced certificate with a note indicating C2-level performance. Clarify this with high-achieving students to avoid confusion on applications.

What students receive

  • A Statement of Results — overall grade plus individual paper scores on the Cambridge Scale
  • A Cambridge English Certificate for Grade A, B, or C
  • A Cambridge English Certificate Supplement with a detailed performance profile

Lesson 1 — Reading & Use of English

Teaching Use of English (Parts 1–4)

The Use of English tasks test candidates' ability to manipulate language at word, sentence, and discourse level. The most effective approaches for each part:

Part 1 — Multiple Choice Cloze: Building Collocation Awareness

The most common error is choosing a word that seems right semantically but doesn't collocate. Students often confuse do/make, bring/take, or overlook fixed prepositional phrases.

Classroom activities:

  • Collocation mapping: Give students a set of nouns (e.g. decision, progress, mistake, effort) and ask them to map which verbs collocate. Then practise in context.
  • Distractor analysis: After a practice test, analyse wrong answers together — can students explain why the distractor doesn't work?
  • Lexical notebooks: Encourage students to record new vocabulary in collocation pairs, not in isolation.
Part 2 — Open Cloze: Grammar in Context

Part 2 tests grammatical awareness that students may have internalised imperfectly. Common gap types: determiners, pronouns, auxiliary verbs, prepositions, conjunctions, and linkers.

  • Dependent preposition drills: Regular practice with verb/adjective/noun + preposition combinations in context (e.g. depend on, keen on, aware of, result in).
  • Discourse marker focus: Use authentic texts with connectors removed — students fill in and justify their choices.
  • Sentence completion dictation: Dictate sentences with gaps at the position of function words — builds automaticity.
Part 3 — Word Formation: Systematic Affix Study

Students need to know (1) what the correct word class is for the gap, and (2) how to form it. Many errors come from students knowing the root but applying the wrong suffix, or forgetting negative prefixes.

Common prefixes
un-, in-, im-, ir-, dis-, mis-, over-, under-, re-
Noun suffixes
-tion, -ness, -ity, -ment, -ance, -ence, -er, -ist, -ship
Adj. suffixes
-ful, -less, -able, -ible, -ive, -ous, -al, -ic, -ish
Adv. suffixes
-ly (most common), -ward(s), -wise

Classroom activity: Word family grids — give students root words and have them complete a grid: verb / noun / adjective / adverb / negative form. This builds the metalinguistic awareness needed for Part 3.

Part 4 — Key Word Transformations: Grammar Drilling

Common structures tested:

  • Passives (including perfect and modal passives)
  • Reported speech and reporting verbs (suggest, recommend, insist, accuse)
  • Third conditional and mixed conditionals
  • Comparative and superlative structures
  • Modal verbs for past deduction and criticism
  • Phrasal verbs and their equivalent single-word verbs

Teaching Reading (Parts 5–8)

Part 5 — Reading for Nuance

Students frequently choose the wrong option because it contains vocabulary lifted directly from the text. Train them to look for paraphrase rather than matched wording. The correct answer is almost always a paraphrase; distractors use text vocabulary misleadingly.

Part 6 — Cross-Text Multiple Matching

The most cognitively demanding part. Students must hold four writers' views in mind simultaneously and compare them on specific points.

Text-coding system:

  • Before reading: identify the topic and sub-topics likely to appear
  • While reading: code each text — mark the writer's stance on each sub-topic with +/−/? symbols
  • For each question: identify which two or three writers could be candidates, then read closely to confirm or eliminate
Part 7 — Gapped Text: Understanding Cohesion

Teach students to look for three types of clues:

  • Reference words: pronouns and demonstratives (e.g. this idea, they, such problems)
  • Lexical chains: words in the same semantic field that link sections
  • Discourse markers: connectors that signal a logical relationship (however, therefore, furthermore)

Also teach students to check the paragraph after the gap — the removed paragraph must connect logically to both sides.

Part 8 — Multiple Matching: Efficient Scanning

Students should read all 10 questions first to identify keywords, then scan rather than read — locating sections by keyword proximity and then reading closely to confirm.

Lesson 2 — Listening

Teaching Approach: Before, During, and After

Effective listening instruction follows a three-phase approach. Most classroom time should be spent on the pre-listening and post-listening phases — the exam tests whether this preparation pays off during listening.

Before listening
Predict topic and vocabulary; read questions carefully; identify what information is needed
During listening
Listen for gist first, then detail; don't fixate on missed points; note key information
After listening
Use second listening to confirm/correct; analyse distractors; review scripts

Part-by-Part Teaching Strategies

Part 1 — Short Extracts (Multiple Choice)

Three extracts, two questions each. The extracts can be monologues or interactions in a variety of formal and informal registers.

Common errors: Students choose options containing words they heard, even when the meaning doesn't match. Distractors are designed to exploit this.

Strategy: Before listening, students predict: What type of text is this? What is the speaker's likely purpose? What vocabulary might appear? Then during listening, they focus on meaning, not word matching.

Part 2 — Sentence Completion (Accuracy Under Pressure)

A long monologue of 3–4 minutes with 8 gaps to complete. The answer is a word or short phrase heard directly in the recording — no paraphrase required.

Key skills: Prediction (read all 8 sentences before listening to anticipate what type of information is needed); note-taking (write clearly during the first listening, check and complete during the second).

Spelling: Minor spelling errors are accepted if the word is recognisable. Train students to write neatly and spell common words correctly.

Part 3 — Discussion (Identifying Attitudes and Opinions)

An interview or conversation between two or more speakers, with 6 multiple-choice questions requiring candidates to identify attitudes, opinions, and reasons.

Common error: Choosing the option that matches the first thing a speaker says, rather than their final position after discussion. Train students to listen for discourse markers that signal a change of view: on the other hand, having said that, although, I now think…

Part 4 — Multiple Matching (Two Simultaneous Tasks)

Five short monologues on a related theme. Candidates must match each speaker to two separate sets of five options. Cognitively the most demanding listening task.

Strategy: In the 45 seconds before the recording begins, read all ten options (both tasks A and B). During the first listening, attempt both tasks. Use the second listening to confirm Task A first, then check Task B.

⚠️

Key insight from examiners

Students who understand the text but get the wrong answer are usually falling for distractors — they hear related vocabulary but don't process the speaker's actual meaning or stance.

Lesson 3 — Writing

The Four Assessment Criteria

Every writing task is marked on the same four criteria, each on a scale of 0–5, giving a maximum of 20 marks per task.

Content
All task points addressed; ideas relevant, developed, and accurate to the prompt
Comm. Achievement
Correct genre conventions; appropriate register; positive effect on target reader
Organisation
Clear, logical structure; effective cohesive devices; appropriate paragraphing
Language
Wide range of vocabulary and grammar; accuracy; natural-sounding expression

Part 1 — The Discursive Essay

Two input texts present different viewpoints on a topic. The essay prompt asks candidates to write a discursive essay that discusses both views and gives their own opinion. The essay must be 220–260 words.

Structure framework
  • Introduction: Introduce the topic broadly; acknowledge the debate (2–3 sentences)
  • Body paragraph 1: Discuss/evaluate View 1 with supporting arguments
  • Body paragraph 2: Discuss/evaluate View 2, or present a counterargument
  • Conclusion: Summarise and state a clear personal opinion or recommendation
💡

Common content error

Students simply summarise the two input texts rather than engaging critically with the arguments. The task requires them to evaluate the views — not just report them.

Part 2 — Task Types

📧 Email/Letter ▼

Requires consistent appropriate register — formal or semi-formal depending on the prompt. Key features: clear greeting and sign-off; well-organised paragraphs; all bullet points addressed. A common error is mixing formal and informal register within the same letter.

📋 Report ▼

A report has a clear purpose and target reader (usually a manager or committee). Features: a heading/title, section headings, objective tone, recommendations section. Students should avoid writing a report like an essay — the structure should make it easy to scan.

💼 Proposal ▼

Similar to a report but forward-looking — it proposes a course of action. Must be persuasive as well as factual. Requires headings, a rationale, and concrete recommendations with justification.

⭐ Review ▼

A review (book, film, restaurant, product, service) evaluates something for a target reader. Should include both description and evaluation, with a clear recommendation. Personal voice and engaging language are valued — but must remain appropriate to the target publication.

✅

Lesson 4 — Speaking

The Five Assessment Criteria

Grammar & Vocab
Range and accuracy of structures and lexis
Discourse Management
Coherence, extended turns, and relevant contributions
Pronunciation
Intelligibility, stress, intonation, and individual sounds
Interactive Comm.
Initiating, responding, developing the conversation collaboratively
Global Achievement
Overall effectiveness of communication across the whole test

Teaching Each Part

Part 1 — Interview: Extending Answers

The interlocutor asks straightforward personal questions. The error most students make is giving short, factual answers. At C1 level, responses should be extended — students should develop their answers with reasons, examples, and personal reflection.

Classroom drill: Expand-and-extend — give students a simple question (e.g. Do you enjoy travelling?). First they answer in one sentence. Then they expand to three sentences. Then to six. This builds the habit of extending naturally.

Part 2 — Long Turn: Comparing, Speculating, and Evaluating

Each candidate speaks for about one minute comparing two photographs and answering a follow-up question.

The structure students need:

  • Open: Describe what you can see briefly (Both images show…)
  • Compare: Identify similarities and differences (While the first image…, the second…)
  • Speculate: Go beyond what you can see (It could be that… / The person might be feeling…)
  • Evaluate: Answer the specific question (I think this relates to… because…)

Language bank for speculation: It looks as though… / This could suggest… / I imagine… / There's a sense of… / It's hard to tell, but perhaps…

Part 3 — Collaborative Task: Negotiating Together

Two stages: (1) Discussion (2 min) — respond to the central question using all or most of the visual prompts; (2) Decision (1 min) — reach a joint conclusion.

Language for interaction:

  • Inviting: What do you think about…? / Do you agree?
  • Building: That's a good point. I'd also add that… / Similarly…
  • Disagreeing politely: I see what you mean, but… / I'm not sure I agree entirely because…
  • Negotiating: Shall we go with…? / I think we could both agree that…
⚠️

Common classroom problem

One candidate dominates and the other barely speaks. Interactive Communication is assessed on both candidates. Explicitly teach inviting language and monitor for balance in all speaking practice.

Part 4 — Discussion: Depth and Range

The interlocutor asks follow-up questions related to the Part 3 theme. This is an opportunity to demonstrate the full range of grammar and vocabulary, and sophisticated discourse management through extended, well-organised responses.

Teach students to use: discourse markers for structure (firstly, moreover, in contrast); hedging language (it could be argued that… / it's worth considering…); and abstract vocabulary appropriate to C1 level.

Lesson 1 — Classroom Warmers

Effective warmers do two things: they activate learners and they connect directly to the lesson's target skill. Random warmers waste time. Well-designed warmers prime the vocabulary, grammar, or skill you'll practise in the main activity.

Principles of an effective CAE warmer

  • Maximum 8–10 minutes — warmers should energise, not exhaust
  • Low stakes: no failure modes, everyone participates
  • Skill-linked: explicitly connects to the lesson focus
  • Language recycling: revisits vocabulary or structures from previous lessons
🟦 Warmer 1 — Word Association Race (Use of English focus) ▼

Aim: Activate collocation and vocabulary knowledge before Parts 1–3.

Time: 5–7 minutes

How it works: Write a root word or topic on the board (e.g. ENVIRONMENT). Students have 2 minutes in pairs to write as many collocating words, phrases, or word-forms as possible. Pairs share their lists — the pair with the most correct items wins. Teacher then uses the lists to pre-teach key vocabulary for the lesson.

🟩 Warmer 2 — Spot the Distractor (Reading focus) ▼

Aim: Build awareness of how distractors work in Parts 5 and 8.

Time: 7–8 minutes

How it works: Prepare a short paragraph from an authentic text. Write four comprehension statements — three that accurately paraphrase the text and one that uses words from the text but misrepresents the meaning. Students identify the distractor and explain why it's wrong. A direct rehearsal of the Part 5 skill.

🟨 Warmer 3 — 60-Second Speak (Speaking focus) ▼

Aim: Warm up fluency and confidence before Speaking practice.

Time: 6–8 minutes

How it works: Give students a discussion prompt on a card (e.g. What is the most important quality in a leader?). Student A speaks for 60 seconds without stopping. Student B then adds to, disagrees with, or asks a follow-up question. Simulates Parts 3 and 4 interaction in a low-stakes format.

🟧 Warmer 4 — Affix Challenge (Word Formation focus) ▼

Aim: Revise word formation before Part 3 practice.

Time: 5–6 minutes

How it works: Show 5–6 root words (e.g. RELY, CARE, MANAGE, RESPONSIBLE). Students work in pairs to write as many word forms as possible (noun, verb, adjective, adverb, negative) in 90 seconds. Review together and highlight forms commonly tested in Part 3.

Lesson 4 — Listening: Lesson Planning

Effective listening lessons for CAE invest time before and after the recording — not just during. Pre-listening prediction and post-listening distractor analysis are the two highest-value phases; most teachers spend too little time on both.

Lesson 5 — Speaking Part 2: Long Turn

The long turn is the part where students most often run out of things to say after 20–30 seconds. This framework builds the linguistic scaffolding they need to sustain extended monologue for a full minute.

Stage 1 — Noticing (10 min)

Play or describe a sample long turn (the Raphael & Maude speaking test in Module 1 is ideal — jump to Part 2). Ask: How does the candidate open their response? How do they move from description to comparison to speculation? What language signals each stage?

Stage 2 — Language Input (15 min)
FunctionExample phrases
Opening / describingBoth images show… / In the first image, I can see… / These photos both depict…
ComparingWhile the first image… the second… / Both images share… / Unlike the first photo…
SpeculatingIt looks as though… / This could suggest… / I imagine the person might be feeling… / Perhaps the reason is…
EvaluatingOverall, I think… / The connection to [theme] is… / This relates to the question because…
Stage 3 — Controlled Practice (15 min)

Provide two photographs on a topic. In pairs, Student A attempts the long turn using the language table as a scaffold. Student B listens and tracks: How long did A speak? Did they cover all four stages? What speculative language did they use?

Stage 4 — Feedback and Repeat (15 min)

Pairs swap and re-attempt with a different image set. Feedback focuses on the language used and the four-stage structure. Many students need 3–4 attempts before extending naturally to a full minute.

Lesson 6 — Speaking Parts 3 & 4

Parts 3 and 4 assess collaboration and discussion. The key teaching focus is Interactive Communication — students must learn to do more than express their own opinions; they must build, negotiate, and respond.

Stage 1 — Discourse Function Analysis (15 min)

Show a transcript of a strong Part 3 exchange (the Raphael & Maude video in Module 1 shows Parts 3 & 4 — use that transcript, or a printed Part 3 sample from the Cambridge Handbook). Have students identify and categorise each utterance: opening, inviting, agreeing, disagreeing, building, negotiating, concluding. They will notice how many turns are interactive rather than monologic.

Stage 2 — Language Categorisation (15 min)

Students sort a set of 20–25 phrases into categories: inviting, agreeing, disagreeing politely, building on ideas, hedging, negotiating. Pair work, with reference list provided.

Stage 3 — Mini-Discussion Practice (20 min)

Pairs receive a Part 3 prompt and 5 visual stimuli. They have 2 minutes to discuss using as many of the target phrases as possible. Observer (other student or teacher) ticks each phrase used. Feedback focuses on what was missing and why.

Stage 4 — Part 4 Extension (10 min)

The same pairs answer Part 4 follow-up questions, demonstrating extended responses and discourse markers (firstly, on the other hand, having said that). Focus on individual depth as well as interaction.

  • Use official sample papers effectively across the course — diagnostic, skills-building, and mock

Lesson 1 — What Is Assessment Literacy?

Assessment literacy refers to a teacher's deep understanding of how an exam works: what it tests, how it is marked, what distinguishes one grade band from another, and how to communicate this knowledge to students in ways that improve their performance.

An assessment-literate C1 Advanced teacher can:

  • Look at a student's writing and identify specifically which criterion is limiting their score — not just "it needs improvement"
  • Listen to a student's speaking and distinguish a Discourse Management weakness from a Grammar & Vocabulary weakness
  • Explain to a student why their answer for Part 6 is wrong even though they correctly identified the writer's view
  • Design lessons that target the specific gap between a student's current performance and the next grade band

Why it matters: the research case

Research in language assessment consistently shows that students who understand the marking criteria perform significantly better than those who only know "do well in the exam". When students can self-assess against the criteria, they develop metacognitive awareness — they know why a response is strong or weak, not just whether they got it right.

Putting it into practice

1. Share the criteria with students from day one

Give students the four Writing criteria (Content, Communicative Achievement, Organisation, Language) at the start of your course — not a week before the exam. Use them as a shared language for all feedback throughout the year.

2. Use student-friendly translations

The official criteria use technical language. Translate them:

Official criterionStudent-friendly version
Content"Have you answered every part of the question with relevant, developed ideas?"
Communicative Achievement"Does this sound like the right type of text for the right reader, in the right register?"
Organisation"Can the reader follow your ideas easily? Are your paragraphs logical and well-linked?"
Language"Are you using a wide range of vocabulary and grammar accurately — not just safe, simple language?"
3. Peer and self-assessment against the criteria

Have students mark each other's writing using a simplified version of the criteria before you mark it. Then compare their assessment with yours. The discussion of disagreements is more valuable than agreement — it reveals misunderstandings about what the criteria really mean.

💡

Key principle

The goal of assessment literacy is not for students to "game the exam" — it is for them to understand what good language use looks like at C1 level and internalise those standards in their own production.

Lesson 2 — Writing Assessment in Depth

The Marking Scale: 0–5 per Criterion

Each of the four writing criteria is marked on a scale of 0–5. A mark of 3 represents adequate performance — meeting the requirements without distinction. A mark of 4–5 indicates performance that is clearly effective (4) or very effective with minimal error (5).

MarkDescriptor
5Fully achieves the intended effect; very wide range; minimal errors; reader fully engaged
4Clearly achieves the effect; good range; errors do not impede communication
3Adequately achieves the effect; reasonable range; some errors but meaning clear
2Inconsistently achieves the effect; limited range; errors sometimes impede communication
1Rarely achieves the effect; very limited range; frequent errors impede communication
0Insufficient language to assess, or completely irrelevant content

Reading Each Criterion Carefully

Content (0–5)

Ask: Has every required element of the task been addressed with relevant, developed ideas?

Common problems:

  • Only addressing one of two required viewpoints in Part 1 (automatic content ceiling at 3)
  • Inventing information not related to the prompt
  • Writing too little — under 220 words means key content is likely missing
Communicative Achievement (0–5)

Ask: Does the text achieve its communicative purpose and use the right conventions for its genre and reader?

Penalises:

  • Register inconsistency — shifting between formal and informal within the same text
  • Generic writing — an essay written in the style of a report, or a review reading like a formal essay
  • Lack of reader engagement — technically accurate but failing to achieve its purpose

Teaching activity: Give students two versions of the same opening paragraph — one with consistent appropriate register and one with a jarring shift. Ask them to identify the shift and rewrite it.

Organisation (0–5)

Ask: Can the reader follow the argument or narrative easily? Is it well-structured and cohesive?

Effective organisation at C1 includes:

  • Clear paragraph structure with one main idea per paragraph
  • A range of cohesive devices — not just firstly / secondly / finally
  • Topic sentences that preview the paragraph's argument
  • Concluding sentences that summarise or link to the next paragraph

Students who only use firstly, secondly, in conclusion are typically capped at 3 for Organisation. Teach a wider range: Furthermore / In addition / By contrast / As a result / This is illustrated by / Notwithstanding this…

Language (0–5)

Ask: Does the candidate use a wide range of vocabulary and grammatical structures accurately?

At C1 level, examiners are looking for:

  • Evidence of complex grammatical structures attempted and used accurately
  • Sophisticated vocabulary — not just correct, but precise and varied
  • Low error rate — occasional slips accepted at Grade C; Grade A requires near-accuracy
⚠️

The "safe language trap"

Many students write in safe, simple language to avoid errors. This strategy caps them at Grade C for Language. Teach students to take controlled risks — attempt complex structures and high-level vocabulary even if imperfectly, because range matters as much as accuracy at C1.

How to Use the Teacher Guide for Marking Practice

The Teacher Guide contains annotated student responses for each task type at different grade bands. Use them as follows:

  1. Mark blind first: read a sample and assign your marks for each criterion before reading the examiner's commentary
  2. Compare: check your marks against the official marks; note discrepancies
  3. Read the commentary: understand why the examiner gave the marks they did, especially where you disagreed
  4. Re-mark: apply your revised understanding to another sample response

Repeat across at least three full tasks per task type to develop reliable marking consistency.

Lesson 3 — Listening Assessment & Using Answer Keys

How Listening Is Marked

Unlike Writing and Speaking, Listening is marked objectively — each item has one correct answer. However, understanding why specific answers are correct (and why distractors are wrong) is essential for effective teaching.

Part 2: Sentence Completion — Marking Rules

This part has specific rules teachers should know:

  • The answer must come directly from what is heard — paraphrase is not accepted
  • Answers should be concise — typically 1–3 words
  • Minor spelling errors are accepted if the word is still recognisable
  • Answers must be grammatically correct within the sentence — numbers, dates, and proper nouns are common answer types

Using Answer Keys Diagnostically

The Cambridge answer keys do more than give correct answers — they show the range of acceptable responses for each item. For sentence completion, acceptable alternatives may be listed. For multiple choice and multiple matching, only one answer is listed, but the reasoning appears in the listening scripts (Handbook or Teacher Guide).

Diagnostic technique: After a listening practice task, don't just mark and move on. For every wrong answer, ask: Was this a failure to hear? A failure to understand? Or a distractor trap? Each requires a different teaching response:

Error typeCauseTeaching response
Failure to hearSpeed, accent, connected speechPhonology work; dictation; slowed audio practice
Failure to understandVocabulary or discourse knowledge gapVocabulary pre-teaching; schema building
Distractor trapChose answer with heard words, not matching meaningDistractor analysis; meaning-focus training

Official Answer Keys

Use these answer keys after completing the corresponding sample paper components. They give you the marks; the diagnostic value comes from comparing your students' wrong answers against the type-of-error framework above.

Lesson 4 — Sample Papers & Computer-Based Preparation

Using Sample Papers Effectively

Sample papers are not just for mock exams — they are the most authentic preparation resource available. Used thoughtfully, a single sample paper can provide material for multiple lessons.

Diagnostic use (before teaching)

Administer a sample paper (or individual components) as a diagnostic at the start of the course. Analyse results by paper and by part to identify:

  • Which papers students are strongest and weakest in overall
  • Which specific parts cause the most difficulty across the class
  • Whether errors cluster around specific skill types (e.g. all students struggle with Part 6 but not Part 5)

Use this data to prioritise teaching focus over the course.

Skills-building use (during the course)

Rather than using a full paper in one sitting, use individual parts as skills-building tasks integrated into topic-based lessons. For example: use the Part 5 reading text from the sample paper as input for a vocabulary lesson, then use the questions to practise the multiple choice strategy.

Mock exam use (near the exam)

In the final 4–6 weeks, administer a full timed mock exam using one complete sample paper per session. This builds:

  • Time management skills across each paper
  • Exam stamina (particularly important for the 90-minute Reading & UoE paper)
  • Familiarity with the exam environment and pacing

Computer-Based Exam Preparation

For students taking the computer-based version, there are specific practical differences to prepare for. The content is identical — but the interface requires specific habits.

Reading & Use of English on screen
  • Scrolling long texts: Parts 5 and 8 texts can be long. Train students to scroll and track their position without losing their place. Practise with long texts on any device.
  • Highlighting: The CB version allows text highlighting. Train students to use this strategically — highlight reference words and key phrases rather than entire paragraphs.
  • Part 4: Students type their transformation directly. Remind them that the word count is visible — this helps them check the 3–6 word limit.
Writing on screen
  • Word count tool: The CB version shows a live word count. Train students to target 230–255 words (the sweet spot within 220–260) rather than stopping at exactly 220.
  • No spell-check: The word processor does not include autocorrect or spell-check. Students must proofread manually — make this a habit in all written work.
  • Cut and paste: Students can move sentences and paragraphs within their response. Train them to plan before writing so they rarely need to restructure on screen.
Listening on screen
  • Audio plays through headphones — students should practise listening with headphones to replicate exam conditions
  • For Part 2, students type their answers directly. Practise typing short answers quickly and accurately under time pressure.
⚠️

Critical reminder

Students who have only practised on paper and sit a computer-based exam are at a disadvantage — not because the language content is different, but because the interface is unfamiliar. Integrate screen-based practice throughout the course, not just before the exam.

Lesson 1 — Principles of Effective Self-Access Learning

What is self-access learning?

Self-access learning means students working independently through resources — without a teacher present — to develop skills or practise language. At C1 level, learners have the cognitive maturity and language awareness to benefit greatly from well-designed self-access materials, provided they know how to use them.

The key distinction between effective and ineffective self-access is metacognitive engagement: is the student simply completing the task, or are they reflecting on their process, identifying their errors, and applying strategies?

The teacher's role in self-access

Simply handing students a resource and saying "work through this" rarely produces good outcomes. Effective self-access requires three teacher inputs:

1. Orientation
Explain the purpose, how it connects to class work, and the specific strategy to apply
2. Structure
Provide a clear task framework: what to do, in what order, and how long for each stage
3. Follow-up
Create a mechanism for students to report back — even briefly — so accountability is maintained

Principles of good self-access material

The Cambridge English self-access materials are designed with these principles:

  • Strategy-first: each resource explains the strategy before presenting the practice task
  • Guided discovery: rather than telling students what to do, materials guide them to notice features of effective language use themselves
  • Self-assessment: most materials include a checklist or reflection question helping students evaluate their own response against the criteria
  • Incremental difficulty: tasks move from supported (with scaffolding) to independent (without scaffolding) within the same resource

When self-access works best

  • The student has a clear goal — not "improve my English" but "practise speculating in Speaking Part 2 for 20 minutes"
  • The resource matches the student's current level — too easy produces no growth; too hard produces frustration
  • There is an accountability mechanism — a brief written reflection or self-assessment checklist submitted to the teacher
  • It follows, or precedes, related classroom instruction — isolated self-access without classroom connection is far less effective
💡

Key research finding

Students who combine classroom instruction with structured self-access practice (at least 2–3 hours per week between lessons) consistently outperform those who only practise in class. The mechanism is not just more practice time — it is the development of independent learning skills that transfer to exam conditions.

Lesson 2 — The Cambridge Self-Access Materials in Detail

Cambridge English has produced dedicated self-access learning materials for each paper of C1 Advanced. Each is designed for independent student use. Below is a guide to each one — what it contains and how to use it most effectively in your teaching.

Reading & Use of English

Part 5 — Multiple Choice Reading

Contains: A complete Part 5 task with strategy guidance integrated throughout. Students are shown how to approach each question systematically before attempting it.

Best use: Assign as pre-work before a lesson focused on Part 5. Ask students to complete the strategy notes and bring their reasoning to class. Discuss: Did they follow the strategy? Did it help? Where did they still go wrong?

Key takeaway: The correct answer paraphrases the text; distractors use text vocabulary misleadingly.

Part 6 — Cross-Text Multiple Matching

Contains: A guided walkthrough of the cross-text matching process with a complete practice task. Includes a text-coding technique (marking each writer's stance before answering questions).

Best use: Use this as a remediation tool after a poor class performance on Part 6. Then revisit in the next lesson with a new Part 6 practice task.

Key takeaway: Systematically code each writer's stance before looking at the questions — do not attempt to answer from memory.

Listening

Part 1 — Multiple Choice

Contains: A complete Part 1 task with pre-listening strategy guidance and post-listening reflection questions.

Best use: Assign after teaching listening strategies in class. The reflection questions ask students to identify which distractors they fell for and why — bring these reflections to class for discussion.

Key takeaway: Focus on meaning and speaker purpose, not matching vocabulary from the options.

Writing

Part 1 — Discursive Essay

Contains: Strategy guide for the discursive essay, a complete Part 1 task, a model answer with examiner commentary, and a self-assessment checklist students complete after writing their own response.

Best use: Ideal for flipped classroom delivery. Students read the strategy guide and study the model at home, then bring their written response to class for peer feedback using the criteria.

Key takeaway: The self-assessment checklist after writing is the learning moment — if students skip it, they miss the metacognitive benefit.

Speaking

Part 2 — Long Turn

Contains: Language banks for describing, comparing, speculating, and evaluating; practice photographs; a model response with commentary; and a self-assessment grid based on the Speaking criteria.

Best use: Students record themselves attempting the long turn, then listen back while completing the self-assessment grid. This self-monitoring technique is one of the most effective independent speaking-development tools available.

Key takeaway: Students are often surprised by what they hear when they listen back — this is the awareness-raising moment that drives improvement.

Lesson 3 — Integrating Self-Access Into Your Programme

Model 1 — The Structured Homework Programme

The most straightforward integration model: each week's self-access material is directly linked to that week's classroom skill focus.

1Reading Part 5 strategySelf-Access Part 5Discuss distractor analysis
2Listening Part 1 strategiesSelf-Access Listening Part 1Students share reflection notes
3Writing discursive essaySelf-Access Writing Part 1Peer assessment using criteria
4Speaking long turnSelf-Access Speaking Part 2In-class replay of recordings
5Reading Part 6 cross-textSelf-Access Part 6Compare text-coding strategies
✅

Ask students for a 3-sentence reflection after each self-access task: (1) What strategy did I use? (2) Where did I go wrong? (3) What will I do differently next time? Collect at the start of the following lesson — 2 minutes to review, valuable diagnostic data.

Model 2 — Flipped Classroom

Students encounter new content and strategy guidance at home before the lesson. Class time is reserved for application, practice, and feedback.

  1. Before class: Students read the strategy section and study the model answer
  2. In class: Brief comprehension check (2–3 questions on the strategy), then directly into timed practice
  3. Peer feedback: Students assess each other's responses using the criteria; teacher gives class-wide feedback on patterns
  4. Consolidation: Students identify one specific thing to improve and set a goal for next independent practice

The flipped model works particularly well for Writing and Speaking — both benefit from maximum practice time in class, not strategy delivery.

Model 3 — Differentiated Independent Study

Not all students have the same weaknesses. Use diagnostic test results to assign individual materials:

Student profileRecommended self-access focus
Strong grammar; weak vocabulary rangeVocabulary similes lesson + collocation practice from the self-access pack
Strong Writing content; weak OrganisationSelf-Access Writing Part 1 — focus on the cohesion checklist
Good fluency; weak Interactive CommunicationSelf-Access Speaking Part 2 — self-assessment grid & inviting language
Struggles with Part 6 cross-text matchingSelf-Access Part 6 — text-coding technique guided practice

Model 4 — Exam Countdown Plan (Final 6–8 Weeks)

In the final weeks, self-access shifts from skills development to consolidation and confidence building:

Weeks before examFocusSelf-access activity
8–7Reading & Use of English revisionSelf-Access Parts 5 and 6; timed Part 7 and 8 practice
6–5Writing consolidationSelf-Access Writing Part 1; one Part 2 task per week
4–3Listening and SpeakingSelf-Access Listening Part 1; Speaking Part 2 with self-recording
2Full mock exam (timed)Sample Papers Set 1 — complete under exam conditions
1Targeted review + confidenceReview mock errors; self-access for weakest areas only
⚠️

Avoid over-testing in the final week

The last week should consolidate known strengths and address specific, manageable weaknesses — not expose students to new weaknesses that cannot be fixed in time. Confidence matters.
